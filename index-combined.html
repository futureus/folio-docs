What is FutureFolio?

FutureFolio is a next-generation magazine delivery platform for tablets and smartphones, offering a wide spectrum of functionality that can fit a variety of use cases.

The platform is made up of three parts: a store front, digital replicas and interactive editions. FutureFolio allows you to mix and match digital replicas and interactive editions whenever and however you need within a single store front, which allows you to build a shared subscriber base regardless of what content you deliver.

If you have questions about the platform that are not covered within this document, please email stefan.kruger@futurenet.com.

Digital replicas

A digital replica is the closest option available from FutureFolio to a print magazine. It is also the fastest and easiest digital magazine type to create and in most cases requires little or no additional modifications to a print publication in order to create. Digital replicas are displayed using the PDF file format that most publishers are well acquainted with.

A digital replica in action.

Magazines created in this format use a simple page turner interface that enables readers to browse, read and zoom in. If possible, FutureFolio will also automatically allow readers to search inside your PDFs, if your text is stored in an easily read format.

FutureFolio also enables the addition of interactive elements and features to page turner issues. This however requires some additional setup which is outlines in the Adding Interactivity section.

What's in this section:

  1. Optimising your PDFs - ensure digital issues display correctly and are true to their print counterparts.
  2. Creating page thumbnails - engage your readers while the page loads and is ready for zooming with preview thumbnails.
  3. Packaging your issue - once your assets are complete you will need to prepare them for usage.
  4. Adding interactivity - create quick and simple interactive features without extra man-hours.
    • Page jumping - create a more fluid experience with interactive contents pages and buttons.
    • Showing a slideshow - display swipe-able image slideshows without interrupting the issue's page numbering and flow.
    • Playing video - add trailers and videos to your pages.
    • Playing audio - create a simple audio playlist or just play the odd audio effect.
    • Launching a HD page - get the best of both worlds by adding single HD pages to your digital replica.
  5. Adding web pages - send readers to external websites with standard PDF weblinks

Optimising your PDFs

To ensure your digital replica will run on all the currently supported iPhone and iPad devices and display correctly, your pages PDF files will need to be exported correctly.

Digital replicas are created by exporting each page of the publication as a separate PDF file. For optimal performance you will need to export your PDFs with the following setting:

  • Export a single page PDF of every page of the publication
  • The pages should be laid out as single pages and not spreads
  • Starting from 0, pages need to conform to the page-0.pdf naming convention (page-0.pdf, page-1.pdf, page-2.pdf....etc)
  • Export to PDF version 1.4
  • The PDF should not be flattened or repurposed from a flattened file
  • Colour and greyscale images: 150ppi, maximum quality JPEG compression
  • Monochrome images: 300ppi, CCITT Group 4 compression
  • All colours should be converted to sRGB
  • No spot colours
  • No printers marks
  • All print-specific design elements should be omitted
  • Add hyperlink boxes if you wish urls etc to be clickable
  • (Optional) Encrypt each PDF with an unified password
    • Requires a password to open the document
    • Encrypt all document contents
    • If using Acrobat Professional choose Compatibility: Acrobat 7 and later, encryption level 128-bit AES
    • If using Acrobat Professional this can be done by creating a security policy – this is useful because the person applying the security policy doesn’t need to know the password

Colour profiles and you

CMYK is a print colour profile, it will not display correctly on an iPad or iPhone, you will get a dodge effect. To avoid this, ensure you are using the RGB colour profile.

iPhone and iPad devices have a limited amount of hardware resources, this is even more true in lower end older devices. These settings prevent slow load times, potential memory warnings and reduce your issue download sizes while maintaining amazing quality.

Creating page thumbnails

PDF files can take a second or two to render completely, however images can display nearly instantly. But creating low resolution thumbnail files FutureFolio Viewer disguises this delay by displaying something that closely resembles the final page. If you do not have these thumbnail files, your issue will not work.

Like with your PDF files, there are a number of settings that need to be followed in order to create your digital replica:

Thumbnails need to match up

Remember that your thumbnails are the interim display method before the PDF pages have fully loaded. Your thumbnails need to both match the corresponding PDF of the same name and not contain any crop marks or bleed edges that would result in the thumbnail being smaller or bigger than the PDF page.

  • Create an image file for each page of your publication.
  • Starting from 0, thumbnails need to conform to the page-0.jpg naming convention (page-0.jpg, page-1.jpg, page-2.jpg....etc) to match the PDF files.
  • Image files must be saved in the JPEG format using the maximum quality compression (using the .jpg filename extension).
  • The maximum height of your thumbnails needs to be 256 pixels tall. The width can be variable.
  • You must use the colour profile RGB

Packaging your issue

All issues in a FutureFolio container app are downloaded as archived .zip files. For any issue you've created to be downloadable or viewable in a FutureFolio container app you will need to archive your issue files.

Simply place all your PDF, thumbnail, video, sound and image files in a single location along with any directories for fully interactive counterparts. Then select these files (not the folder) and archive them using: File > Compress X items.

This archive is your full issue, you can now test this with the FutureFolio Viewer (see "Viewing in FutureFolio Viewer"). Once you are happy, upload this archive to the filetransfer.future.net.uk server using the credentials supplied to you when your account was first set up.

Adding interactivity

Issues created using the Digital replica technique can too contain interactive elements like the fully featured Interactive editions. These features are restricted to a set list of possible interactive elements (Page jumping, Movies, Sound, Images and HD pages) and are added to your issues through creating link boxes in your PDF files.

The interactive features covered in this section are as follows:

  • Page jumping - Create fully interactive contents pages that jump the user to the correct location.
  • Showing a slideshow - Display images and image slideshows without adding extra pages to your issue.
  • Playing video - Play videos from within the app.
  • Playing audio - You can play music and noises in your issue.
  • Launching a HD page - Embed an Interactive edition page within your Digital replica.

Depending on your desktop publishing tool of choice you will most likely be able to add custom hyperlink boxes to your documents. This is normally done by creating a custom sized box element and adding the link to the entire entity.

For example in Adobe InDesign you need to create an invisible box object and add the link to that via the Hyperlinks pane. To show the Hyperlinks pane click Window > Interactive > Hyperlinks, you can then click "New Hyperlink" to add the clickable link.

All URLs are case-sensitive

Please enter all URLs correctly, iPhone and iPad devices are case sensitive and will fail to load interactivity if any filenames are entered incorrectly.

No extra characters!

If your URL has a space on the end or a return character it will not work. FutureFolio simply reads the link you've provided it doesn't manipulate it. Be sure to check your issues using the FutureFolio Viewer if you are unsure.

Page jumping

Publishers can create an interactive reading experience by linking users to later pages. Using this you can make cover hits jump to the first page of that article or an index page filled with clickable links to start of each section.

To creating a page jump link, add a hyperlink to a region of your PDF and set the url to page://10 (replacing 10 with the page number you want the issue to jump to).

Page numbering and you

The desired page number needs to be actual number from the front cover onwards with the front cover being page one.

Page numbering mismatches

Don't use the page-0.pdf filename numbering for your jump to page hyperlink, the filenames start at 0 while your actual page numbers start at 1. So in the case of page-0.pdf, this is actually page://1

Showing a slideshow

If you want to display a number of images without disturbing the flow and paging of the magazine by adding extra pages for each image you can use an interactive slideshow instead.

Image slideshows appear over the top of the issue. Users tap once to go back to the issue and they return to the page where they left off. Images are centered and resize to fit the device window. They also auto page with the standard Apple page dotting system (if there is more than one image) to display their progress through the issue.

Due to memory limitations of iPad and iPhone devices and their colour profile support, some strict guidelines need to be followed for image specification. They are as follows:

  • FutureFolio only supports PNG and JPG image file formats. JPG files need to use the .jpg extension.
  • Images must be exported in the RGB colour profile (8 Bit).
  • Use 72 DPI, you will get a more accurate representation of the size on the actual device as it is the native resolution of the device.
  • Limit your images to 2048 pixels in any direction, if the image is too large it may cause instability and slow loading times.
  • Name your files uniquely, these will live with your PDF pages and thumbnails. You can't share a filename.
  • For slideshows use the following naming convention: image-1.jpg replacing image with your unique name and the extension with your desired one. Name sequential pages in this fashion increasing the number, eg: myslide-1.jpg, myslide-2.jpg, myslide-3.jpg, etc.

Something to consider

Bear in mind that slideshows load the current, previous and next image all at once to create a seamless user experience when swiping, so limit your image sizes and compression where possible.

Once you have your image/images exported, place them in your issue directory, then create a link box adding media://image-1.jpg as the url (replacing image with your unique name and the extension with your desired one). FutureFolio will work out how many files are in your slideshow automatically and if there is only one image it will just display that without pages.

You can automatically jump to a specific image inside the slideshow by entering an image file other than the -1.jpg filename, for example: media://image-5.jpg, this would jump to the 5th image in the slideshow. In theory you could create a number of buttons automatically jumping to various pages of the slideshow as an alternative navigation method.

Playing video

Displaying a video is incredibly simple in FutureFolio. Videos are also one of the few things you can stream online in a digital replica, therefore you can in effect reduce your issue's download size.

To include a video in your downloaded issue rather than streaming, simply link it up and place the file inside your issue archive.

While file sizes and compression can be somewhat disregarded in favour of online streaming of video content, there are still a small list of requirements that must be met in order to successfully display a video inside a FutureFolio issue. These are device requirements and suggested codecs and compression:

  • FutureFolio only supports MP4, M4V and MOV format video files.
  • Video files should be compressed in the H.264 video codec and AAC Audio codec. As to bit rates and/or quality amounts it is down to user discretion. Please bear in mind though that large files played from the device and not streamed increase the size of your issue.
  • Resolutions need to be sensible as the larger the resolution the larger the file size.
  • Frame Rate is down to preference, however it's suggested you use between 15 and 25 FPS.
  • Use unique filenames as they all live in one location.

To get the video to play you need to add a link box with a link pointing to media://myvideo.mp4, obviously replacing myvideo.mp4 with your video's filename.

You can also stream the video by entering a URL but replacing HTTP with media: media://www.mywebsite.com/myvideos/myvideo.mp4, again replacing myvideo.mp4 with your video's filename and the website address with your own (make sure that your video is uploaded also).

Regarding streaming, please bear in mind that viewing your video takes up bandwidth from your website. If your app becomes popular you may have to buy additional bandwidth from your website hosting provider to continue to serve that video.

Media URLs

FutureFolio does a small amount of sanity checking in the form of looking for the video file extension. If it does not see the MP4, M4V or MOV extension it will not display.

Playing audio

FutureFolio supports the following audio file types: .wav, .caf, .mp3, .aif and .aiff. To add an audio file, include it in your issue archive and create a link in the style of: media://myAudioFile.mp3

As for compression, again this is down to each individual publication, just as long as the output matches one of the supported types mentioned in this section. Please do however bear in mind the impact large files will have on your overall issue download size.

Launching a HD page

If a fully interactive issue is just unsustainable, but you would like to add the odd fully interactive page here and there you can do this by creating a single page issue (see "Interactive editions") and including it as a folder in your issue archive.

To link this fully interactive page up, create a hyperlink and point it at your folder using the following style: media://myFolderName.

Leave the folder structure intact

When embedding a HD page, you need to link to the folder that contains that page's assets, inside that folder should reside a meta folder. Linking to the incorrect location or playing with the folder's internal layout may result in a dead link.

Adding web pages

Linking to a webpage couldn't be easier. Simply create a hyperlink inside your Desktop Publishing Suite and enter the full user including http://. Tapping these links will open the URL via Safari.

Automated weblinks

If you type a website URL inside your copy, most DTP applications will convert these into links for you when you export to PDF. If they don't by default, check your export settings for an option along the lines of "Convert URLs to hyperlinks".

If you're looking to create a magazine specifically for tablets and smartphones, you'll need to use FutureFolio's Creator tool. This is an in-house software package that lets you layout pages with interactivity, test what you've made, and package up the results ready for distribution.

FutureFolio Creator is a large and powerful tool, with many options that let you get your pages working exactly as you want. If this is your first time using the software, please start at "Quick start".

Overview

This section will walk you through the basics of FutureFolio Creator, explaining how the core objects of an issue hang together.

The complete stack of FutureFolio objects, from very largest to very smallest, is as follows:

  • One complete interactive edition of a magazine is called an issue, and, when it ships to readers, is provided as a single .zip file that contains all the text, graphics, movies and other media required to recreate the magazine on their devices.
  • Each issue is made up of one or more sections, such as News, Reviews and Tutorials. Sections are used to split up the table of contents and can also provide navigation around the issue.
  • Each section is made up of one or more pages, so the Tutorials section might have an iPhoto tutorial, a Photoshop tutorial, and a Google Chrome tutorial.
  • Each page is made up of one or more renditions, which define the way the page looks on a device. For example, you might create renditions for tablets in both portrait and landscape orientations.
  • Each rendition is made up of one or more layers, which are the actual items on the page – images, text, movies, and so on.

In practice, Future magazines use only one rendition: tablet portrait or tablet landscape.

Glossary of Terms
  1. 360 - a set of photographs taken of an object from multiple angles that the user can spin around.
  2. Alpha - the level of opacity applied to an object or colour. A value of 1 means "fully visible" and a value of 0 means "fully invisible."
  3. Animation - a fixed image box that changes between various pictures over time.
  4. Chrome - the custom user interface that sits on top of your issue. This is designed to let the user navigate around the issue, and should not be
  5. CSS - the styling language used to format text.
  6. HTML - the formatting language used to mark up text.
  7. Hybrid - this describes an issue that is part digital replica and part interactive edition.
  8. Icon - the graphic that represents your title on users devices when it's shown alongside other apps. This is usually replaced by your latest cover.
  9. Layer - an object on a page, such as an image, text or a movie.
  10. Metadata - any text that describes your magazine and/or issue, such as its title, month, price, as well as a short description of what's in the issue.
  11. Newsstand - Apple's magazine delivery mechanism for iPhone and iPad.
  12. Particle system - a special effect that creates page effects such as smoke, fire and dust.
  13. Rendition - one layout of a page, targeting either tablet portrait, tablet landscape, phone portrait or phone landscape.
  14. Retina - Apple's name for high-resolution graphics. Retina screens (iPhone 4, iPhone 4s and iPad 3) are exactly 2x the size of non-retina screens.
  15. Scratch image - an image box that the user can touch to "scratch" away to reveal something below.
  16. Slideshow - a swipeable gallery of images.
  17. Splash screen - the full-screen logo that is shown when your app launches. This usually only appears for a second.
  18. Stack - a set of pages that are placed vertically above each other.
  19. Wipe image - an image box that the user can touch and swipe to reveal an image below.
  20. XML - the raw data format behind Creator issues. This XML is created for you as you work with the Creator tool.

Issue Directory

Each issue has its own unique directory which can be found inside /Simulator/EditingIssues. All of the assets for that issue should be placed inside the issue's folder. FutureFolio Creator will make this directory for you when you add an issue.

To add an issue, click on Add New Issue -> Create blank issue: "Adding an issue"

If you have created any issue templates, they will be listed in the menu and you can create the new issue from these by selecting the required one.

Adding an issue

To delete an issue, click on Edit then click on the cross for the relevant issue: "Deleting an issue"

To return to the normal issue view once you have finished deleting, click on Done

Deleting an issue

Dropbox

When multiple users are working on your issue at once, Dropbox can be a useful tool to manage the files across your team.

When using Dropbox, your issues are stored on a central server, which syncs with a local copy on each of your team member's machines. When one of your team changes / adds / deletes a file, their changes are sent to the Dropbox server, then pushed out to the rest of the team. This means that all of your team is working on the very latest issue.

Dropbox also offers versioning - if necessary, you can roll a file back to an earlier version if any problems are encountered.

For more information, see the Dropbox website.

Importing Issues

You can import any given issue in your /Simulator/EditingIssues directory into another issue.

This feature can be useful, for example, if you want to work on different parts of your issue in isolation and/or by different users - and bring it all together at a later time.

To do this, open the issue which is to be the result of the merge and then click on the Import button, selecting the issue to import.

Importing an issue

The selected issue will be read, and its pages and sections imported into the currently viewed issue.

For each section in the imported issue, if the viewed issue contains a section of the same name, the pages from the imported section will be added to the end of the corresponding viewed issue's section.

If a given section name exists in the imported issue but not in the viewed issue, the imported section will be added to the end of the viewed issue's sections.

Page names

When importing one issue into another, all page names need to be unique, across both issues.

If any given page name is used in both of the issues, the import request will fail and display an error message.

Import failed - common page names

The import process will copy all of the imported issue's assets into the viewed issue's directory. It will also copy the preview images for the imported pages.

Duplicate assets

For each asset in the imported issue's directory, if the viewed issue's directory contains an asset of the same name, the copy process will skip that asset - the asset in the viewed issue will not be overwritten.

Editing overview

Double-clicking on any page will open the page editor, which is split into three sections: controls, layers and the canvas. As you create layers, they will appear in the layers list in the order they were created.

Clicking the Play button will preview the page as the end-user will see it.

Page settings

With the pages tab selected, select a page and click on the triangle icon on the right of the page layer cell, select settings.

Within this settings panel you can set the following attributes for the page:

  • Name - the name of the page as it appears in your list of pages.
  • Title - this is the title for the page that a user will see when the page appears in search results, either inside or outside or the issue.
  • Description - a brief description for the page. This description is searchable and will show in search results.
  • Share URL - a URL that can be shared by Facebook or Twitter. This is unique to the page and not the issue as a whole.
  • Page type - by default pages are set as editorial. The default configuration for an editorial page is to show the chrome and to appear in the table of contents (TOC). Marking a page as an advert page changes the default settings. An advert page is configured to hide the chrome and not appear in the TOC. You can overwrite either of these default settings to achieve other configurations.
  • Force chrome - setting this to show or hide will set the chrome for that page respectively, irrespective of the page type.
  • Force TOC - setting this to show or hide will set wether that page appears in the TOC, irrespective of the page type.

Creating and naming layers

A single rendition is likely to have many layers, such as pictures and text. Each of these layers appear separately in the layers panel so that you can select and manipulate them individually.

To create a new layer, choose from one of the layer types:

  • Box - a rectangle-shaped container that is useful to group layers together, or to provide scrollable sub-areas on your page.
  • Story - a text box containing HTML and CSS.
  • Image - any picture type such as slideshows or 360s.
  • Movie - a standalone movie object with a thumbnail graphic.
  • Table - a sortable, grouped data listing.
  • Map - a drop-in Google Map that lets you highlight locations and draw routes.
  • Annotation - a picture with user-revealable captions.

When you create any of these, they will appear in the centre of your canvas sized at 256x256 and named (eg) "New image layer."

You can change the name of a layer by pressing and holding on it to bring up the layer settings. This allows you to rename the layer and also optionally assign it a colour for easier location.

You can show the editing panel for the layer by clicking on the cog icon at the right of the cell, or by double-clicking the selected layer.

Placing layers

Once a layer is selected, there are four ways you can position it on the page:

  1. Clicking and dragging the view on the canvas lets you position the layer freely.
  2. Typing X and Y values into the layer controls boxes.
  3. Clicking the four up, down, left and right nudge buttons.
  4. Holding down Ctrl+Alt then pressing the up, down, left and right cursor keys on your keyboard.

You can resize layers by grabbing one of the corner boxes and dragging, or by typing width and height values into the layer controls boxes.

Layer visibility

Layers are visible when they are created, which means they are visible to the user and will receive taps when the user touches the screen. Although layers can be hidden by adding as a behaviour, such as when the reader has tapped on a button, you can also hide them initially so that they start life as invisible.

To toggle a layer's visibility, click the eye button next to it in the layers list. If the layer is a box and contains other layers, they will all be invisible.

Show single layer

Hidden layers will be affected by the show single layer behaviour.

Place Inside

If you wish to produce a copy of a layer on the page, first select it then tap the Duplicate button. Note that duplicated layers are identical to their originals: text retains its styling, images retain their behaviours.

If you want both tables to point at the same data, you can modify one of the tables to point at the data of the other.

You can place a picture behind all the objects on your page by using a background picture. This should be the same aspect ratio of your page, otherwise it will appear stretched.

To set a background picture, click the Background Picture button, then select from the list of pictures in your issue directory. You can remove an existing background picture by selecting "No background image" from the list.

It is more efficient to use background images than to place a full-page image layer containing the same image.

Grid mode

By default, layers positions snap to individual pixels, but for more precise placement and sizing of objects tap the Grid Mode button and choose one of the available grid sizes. While Grid Mode is active, any movement or resizing of layers will be snapped to the grid that appears on the screen.

Locked Mode

Once a page has been laid out exactly how the designer wants it, the canvas can be locked by tapping the Lock button – this will prevent accidental changes from happening to the page.

Locked mode is designed to stop changes to the layout of the page, not its structure. That is, a picture on the canvas can't be moved, but a production editor can change its contents to different pictures.

Locked mode can be toggled by tapping the Spacebar.

You can attach behaviours to renditions in the same was as images, except these will be run as soon as the reader has started reading the page.

For most behaviours, this means the reader will have to scroll to the page and wait a split second before the rendition behaviour begins, however an exception to this is animation pre-runs: if you set a "Run animation script" rendition behaviour to have a time delay of 0, and the animation has a delay and duration of 0, that animation will be executed before the page is seen by the user. This allows you modify the page as soon as it loads.

Rendition behaviours are not run again unless the page is unloaded and reloaded. For example, if the user watches an animation play, swipes to the next page, then swipes back to the animation page, the animation will not be run again; the user must swipe away several pages in order for the behaviour to be run again.

To use this feature, type your layer names into the Autoshow Layers box, separated by commas. For example, "layer1, layer2, layer3".

This feature is commonly used in conjunction with the Show Single Layer behaviour, because that only acts on layers that are marked as invisible. For example, you can place an image on your canvas, mark it as invisible, then use Autoshow Layers to have that image appear when the page is read, but still be affected by Show Single Layer.

For example, you may have several images and a story which you want to be initially hidden, showing as a result of the user tapping a button. Scrolling Box Contents

You can place layers inside a box which are larger than the box itself. When you do this, the child layers will be cut off at the box edges.

You may wish to allow users to scroll within the box so that they can view the content which is initially cut off. To achieve this, click on the Layer Settings (cog) button for the box layer and then choose the required option on the Scroll to fit contents control:

  • Never - the user cannot scroll in either direction. Contents remain cut off.
  • Vertical - the user may scroll vertically, but any horizontal overspill is cut off.
  • Horizontal - the user may scroll horizontally, but any vertical overspill is cut off.
  • Both - the user may scroll vertically and horizontally.

Boxes can be set to paged scrolling both with (Paging dots option) or without paging dots (On option). Simply select the desired scrolling type under the Scroll in pages option. Paging will honour vertical and horizontal scrolling and will snap to the size of your box for each page unit.

Paging dots will only honour the Horizontal scrolling setting in Scroll to fit contents, vertical pages will not show page dotting regardless of your settings.

Box scrolling to the edge

Not that when a user scrolls horizontally within a box layer, if they reach the edge of the content and keep scrolling, the page will pick up the scroll movement. This just means that if they keep going, they may eventually scroll to the next page in the issue.

Page Scroll

By default, when a user scrolls some content inside a box layer, the scrolling is smooth and does not snap to position.

You may wish to use the Scroll in pages control to make the box contents scroll in a "paging" way. With this setting switched on, the box contents scroll in units of the box size - so if the user drags then lets go, the box contents will then dock to the nearest page (where the page size is the size of the box).

To see the paging scroll method in action, just scroll within the Photos app on iPhone / iPad.

Edge Bounce

If you have enabled scrolling on your box layer, you also have the option of whether to enable edge bouncing, with the Enable edge bounce button.

When this is set to "ON", the user can drag the contents inwards from the edge. When they let go, it will bounce back to the edge.

When it is set to "OFF", the user can only drag the contents as far as the edge - they cannot over-scroll it.

When this is set to "ON", the contents will display past the edges of the box so that you can see the full contents (or as much as can be displayed on the page). Note, however, that any interactivity (scrolling etc) will only work within the bounds of the box.

Gradient Values

By default, no gradients are added to boxes and these fields can be left empty.

If you wish to add a gradient layer to a box, you can specify the start and end positions of the gradient and the direction. The values are entered as percentages relative to the size of the box. For a vertical gradient the scale goes form 0% (top) to 100% (middle) and back to 0% (bottom), hence a start value of 20% and end value of 50% would place a gradient over the top 1/5 of the box and the bottom 1/2. When you place a layer over other content on a page, the content under your layer will not receive taps from the user - the layer over the top will intercept them.

You may wish for your layer to ignore user taps and pass them on to the content underneath. To do this, you will need the Pass-through taps box layer option.

If your layer is not a box and not grouped within one, then it will need to be placed inside one in order to use this option. To place your layer inside a box, see "Grouping Elements"

Click on the Layer settings button for the box layer and change the Pass-through taps setting to "ON".

With this setting on, when the user taps within the box, the tap will be passed through to the layers underneath. However, if the user has tapped on a layer inside the box which has its own tap effect, that effect will be performed and the tap will not be passed on.

Add new story layer

HTML and CSS

Creator represents text using HTML (Hyper Text Markup Language), and styles text using CSS (Cascading Style Sheets). These technologies are the ones used to represent and style text on the web. The workings of HTML and CSS is beyond the scope of this manual, but introductory texts on these subjects are readily available on the web for the interested.

Using HTML for text means that the text can be searched, read back by screen reader technology for the visually impaired, copy and pasted and shared on social networks, and using CSS for the styling means that fonts can be changed or resized on the fly (if the designer so wishes). CSS style sheets can also be reused across multiple stories, or even across issues or publications. One consequence of this is that global changes to style properties can be made by changing a single style sheet, rather than for every story.

The advanced mode story editor

The basic mode story editor

For designers familiar with the intricacies of HTML and CSS, the Creator interface allows for direct, raw access to the markup for the ultimate in terms of control. This advanced interface is illustrated in "The advanced mode story editor". The basic interface, shown in "The basic mode story editor", gives WYSIWYG style editing for the most common styling features, such as bold, italics etc.

Importing Text

As seen in "HTML and CSS", story layers can be populated directly by simply typing in the text content in the story editor. This may well be sufficient for short, or static pieces of text. However, when dealing with content that has been commissioned from elsewhere, or content that will change from issue to issue, there are a couple of other ways by which text can be flowed into story layers.

Import body copy

Add source file

The first way is to flow in text from an external file inside the issue directory. Click on the Import body copy... button to bring up a list of all suitable text files within the issue and select the one you need, as illustrated in "Import body copy". This is essentially the same as opening the external file, and copying its content into the story editor and pressing save; subsequent changes to the content of the external file will not be reflected in the contents of the story layer.

The second way is to link the story layer to an external source file. The effect of this is that if the external file's content changes, this will be reflected in the story layer. This is a very powerful feature for pages where the layout remains fixed, but the content changes between issue to issue. See "Add source file"

Variables as source files

If you want to write the contents of a variable into your story box, specify the name of the variable in the Source File box, prefixed with a dollar sign. For example, to have the Score variable printed out, you would use $Score.

If you specify multiple variable names, separated by a comma, FutureFolio will automatically print both the name of the variable and its value, which makes it very easy to create shopping lists.

If you intend to use multiple variable names, you should be prepared to name your variables in a way that will be shown to users (eg "Meat and Dairy" rather than "meat_and_dairy"), and you should use the CSS styles p.FolioVariableName and p.FolioVariableValue to style the output.

Styling

Apply external style sheet

Text inside story layers can be styled in two different ways. Firstly, the basic mode of the story editor provides a WYSIWYG interface for the most common styling operations: bold, italic, underscore, strike-through, superscript, subscript, convert to upper case, capitalise, right align, left align, centre, ordered and unordered lists, font selection and font size and background, foreground and highlight colours, illustrated in "The basic mode story editor". For styling needs beyond those, it is necessary to go into advanced mode, and reference a CSS style sheet directly. Although possible to enter inline CSS, this is generally not the recommended approach, as such styles cannot easily be reused elsewhere. Instead, gather all your CSS styles in a separate file that you drop into your issue directory. Clicking the Style allows you to select the relevant style sheet file from your issue directory, as illustrated in "Apply external style sheet"

Auto Format

Apply Auto Format

When flowing in body copy from an external text file not already marked up as HTML, Creator allows a degree of conversion using its Auto Format options, as indicated by "Apply Auto Format". This will allow links to be properly respected, and also inserting HTML paragraph breaks where the copy contains empty lines. Note that there are limits as to how successful such a conversion will be. Some subsequent manual checking and editing may be required.

Options

Story layer options

Tapping the Behaviours button allows you to set a number of options affecting the behaviour of a story layer, as seen in "Story layer options". The settings are:

  • Allow user to scroll: determines if the story box should be scrollable. This is best switched off for static content that fits in its entirety in the box. Text that has been laid out to fit perfectly into a layout may benefit from a non-scrolling box.
  • Text is user scaleable: determines if the user should be allowed to change the font size. User-scaleable text requires design consideration from the beginning, and normally benefits from being placed in a scrolling box.
  • Enable edge bounce: edge bounce is a feature used in many places in iOS, letting you seemingly "scroll beyond the bounds" of a box, and when you let go, the content "snaps" back into bounds. With edge bounce switched off, scrolling will stop dead at the box boundary.
  • Scrollbars: change the colour of the scroll bar of the story box.
  • Floats over page: normally, a story box is "glued" to the page, meaning that if the page it is housed on moves, the story box moves with it. The Floats over page allows you to alter this behaviour, going from normal scrolling to "pinned static", the latter giving the appearance of a "floating" box under which the page moves on scrolling. Judicious use of this feature can create a "faux" parallax effect on scroll.
  • Dynamic movement: if enabled, this story will respond to accelerometer or physics events. Please consult the chapter Dynamic movement for the specifics relating to Dynamic movement.
  • Gradient values: if set, a gradient overlay will be placed over the story. The direction can be set either vertically or horizontally and the start and end positions of the gradient fade are specified as percentages relative to the size of the story container. For example, a vertical gradient with a start value of 20% and an end value of 50% would look like Story gradients.

Retina

In order for your images to look sharp on retina displays, two images are needed - the normal one and then a second one which is double the dimensions, named with the same name, but suffixed "@2x", e.g. myImage.jpg and myImage@2x.jpg. Retina displays will choose the larger image, while non-retina displays will show the smaller.

If the source image file you provide is large enough, Folio will create the double-sized (retina) version for you at build time - it will scale the source image down to double the required display size (or as large as it can without upscaling) and save this to the build directory as myImage@2x.jpg, then scale to the required display size and save this as myImage.jpg. Therefore, wherever possible, you should provide your images at a size at least double their required display size.

If you have @2x images in your issue directory for any reason, you should never specify them as the file to use in the image layer. Always specify the standard-sized image, and the correct image will then be chosen at runtime.

Note that for standard image layers, you do not need to provide the @2x image yourself - simply supply your source image at double the display size and Folio will do the work for you.

The animation image layer type is used to display a series of images which automatically play back in sequence.

Specify the images to display in the animation with the Filenames button (see "Importing Filenames").

Use the Filenames button to specify the top image first, then the bottom image (see "Importing Filenames").

In the example wipe layer shown ( "Wipe direction: right, left, top, bottom"), the top image is the one with the processing effect, while the bottom image is the plain photo with the word "Original on".

You can enter the direction which the image wipes in with the Direction control. The example shows each of the direction options. Clockwise from top left, these are: right, left, top, bottom.

Wipe direction: right, left, top, bottom

You can set an initial start position for the image wipe by appending a percentage after the direction, for example left 25%.

You can choose to show the image wipe as-is, or with a divider image marking out where the top image extends to. To specify an image to use as a divider, enter the image name (including the file extension) in the Divider image field.

Note that the divider image is displayed at exactly the size of the source image, rather than stretching to fit the image wipe. This is so that the divider can overhang the edges of the image, if required. This means that you must tailor its size and orientation for the display size of the image wipe you want to use it on.

Wipe with divider

Retina divider images

When using divider images, you must manually provide retina versions. The build process creates retina images when you provide a source image which is at least double the size you want it to be displayed at. However, divider images are displayed at the source image size to allow for overhang, as described above. So if you provided a double-sized source image, your divider would simply be displayed at that size. So you will need to provide two copies of your divider images - one at the required size and one at double the size, named with the suffix "@2x", e.g. myDividerImage.png and myDividerImage@2x.png. Place these inside your issue directory as normal, and FutureFolio will select the appropriate one when displaying your divider to the user.

If you do not provide retina versions of your divider images, they will simply look low-res on retina displays.

The final setting for image wipes is the Divider fades toggle. This is only used when a divider image has been specified. When the toggle is set to "ON", the divider will be visible only while the user actively wipes the image. When they let go, the divider fades out.

Carousel

Carousels provide the most advanced way of flicking through series of pictures, and has a number of configuration options that let you control precisely how they work.

In its most basic form, you can create a carousel by adding several pictures to your image and choosing the Carousel image type. You will also need to enter a size in the Max Image Size box, which will be used to size each individual image inside the carousel. This Max Image Size setting will be used to scale your images down proportionately so they are never shown larger than this size.

For Carousel type you can provide one of several options:

  1. Coverflow - images array themselves to the left and right of the main image; the reader can swipe to rotate them in 3D.
  2. Rotary - images are placed in 3D space as if they were hanging from a mobile that the reader can spin around.
  3. Inverted Rotary - the same as Rotary, except the reader is placed inside the mobile
  4. Cylinder - images are mapped into 3D space as if they were wrapped around a cylinder.
  5. Inverted Cylinder - the same as Cylinder, except the reader is placed inside the cylinder.
  6. Wheel - the images are placed flat onto the page, and spun around a central point off screen below.
  7. Inverted Wheel - the same as Wheel, but spun around a central point off screen above.
  8. Time Machine - places images into a 3D stack going off into the distance of the screen; the reader can swipe to zoom through the images.
  9. Inverted Time Machine - the same as Time Machine, but the reader swipes the other way.

The default option is Coverflow.

You can customise the layout of your carousel by adding two keywords to your carousel type: "Wrap" will make the carousel infinite so that when the reader reaches the end it will loop to the beginning, and "Vertical" will rotate the carousel so that it slides vertically – for example, "Inverted Cyclinder Vertical Wrap".

The Max Image Size dictates how big your images appear inside the carousel. You must specify this as "WidthxHeight", eg "500x350". This is required because your image box provides the area in which your carousel works, but the images inside must have their own size.

By default, Carousels work similarly to slideshows: as they are swiped, layers named the same as the carousel with -1, -2, -3 and so on at the end will be shown and hidden as if they are captions. If you wish to disable this, enable "Activate with taps" - the user must now tap an image in the carousel to show its associated caption.

Rotation and Scale

Use the Rotation slider to change the orientation of your image layer. The units are degrees.

Use the Scale slider to increase or decrease your image layer's size. The units are percentage of the image layer's size; so a value of 100 would make your image fit to 100% of the image layer size. A value of 50 would make the image half the size of the image layer.

Fit vs Stretch

The Size button lets you choose the fit-mode of your image in the image layer.

Fit mode honours the aspect ratio of the source image(s). For each image, if the image layer is a different aspect ratio, the image will be displayed as large as possible while still maintaining the aspect ratio via letterboxing or pillarboxing.

Stretch mode forces the image to fill the image layer, disregarding the aspect ratio.

Fit mode, stretch mode

Size mode combined with rotation and scale

To effectively combine size mode changes with non-default rotation and scale settings, it is important to understand how they work together.

The first setting which is applied is the size mode - fit or stretch. So your image is sized within the image layer according to your fit / stretch setting before any rotation or scale is applied.

Lastly, the rotation and scale is applied in one transformation. This transformation is applied to the result of placing the image within the layer using the given size mode.

Flip mode

Use the Flip control to flip your image either horizontally or vertically within your image layer.

Toggle Buttons

Toggle buttons allow you to create behaviours similar to those of radio buttons, they can also be used to control other layers on the page through a simple naming convention.

In its most basic form, you can create a toggle button by adding two pictures under the filenames field on the toggle settings panel. The first image specified will be used to represent the unchecked state of the toggle button and the second for the checked state. A third image is optional, if specified this image will be used to show a highlighted state of the toggle button.

Toggle buttons can be further customised in the following ways:

  1. Group name: by assigning a group name, e.g. "Group 1", toggle buttons within the same group will adopt the known behaviour of radio buttons. Only one toggle button within a group can be in the selected state at any one time, selecting one toggle button will deselect all the others in the same group.
  2. Sound effect: specify the filename of a sound sound file with the file extension. This sound file will then be played every time a toggle button is selected. If the toggle button is not assigned to a group then the sound will play when the toggle button is both selected and deselected.
  3. Initial state: this property dictates the state of the toggle button when it loads on the page. Setting this property to "ON" will show the image for the checked state of the toggle button. The default property is "OFF", if you leave all the toggle buttons within a group set to "OFF" Creator will automatically set the last toggle button in that group to "ON" when the page loads.

Using the naming convention "-on"

You can use toggle buttons to toggle the visibility of other layers on the page by naming layers accordingly. If we have a toggle button named "toggle-button" we can use the state of this toggle button to control the visibility of any other layer on the page named "toggle-button-on". Whenever "toggle-button" is selected "toggle-button-on" will be visible, deselecting "toggle-button" will hide "toggle-button-on". More complex situations can be created by combining this naming convention with the group name property, and nesting layers inside boxes etc.

When using the "-on" naming convention you must set the visibility of all the layers on the page named "layername-on" to hidden. The layer will then show according to the state of the toggle button.

Auto-show

If you use the auto-show layer feature to show a layer that is related to a toggle button you will over-ride the toggle feature for that layer.

Local vs Streaming Movies

Creator allows you to either include a movie file in the issue itself and play it locally or to stream it in from a web server of your choice. There are pros and cons with either approach. Local movies will work even if the device isn't connected to the internet and will always be responsive, but if you use local movies the issue size will be inflated as movie files tend to be large. Table Creation

To create a new table layer, click on the Add table button.

Adding a table

Data for each individual table is stored in its own file within your issue. These are created and managed by FutureFolio Creator via the table editor window.

Once you have created a new table layer, clicking on the layer settings button will bring up a dialog asking you how you want to configure your table - a new blank table ("Create blank table") or copied from another table in that issue ("Create from file").

If you choose to create a table from an existing one, you will be presented with a list of any table files found in that issue. Select the table you want to copy. FutureFolio Creator will copy the selected table to a new file and take you into the table editor where you can make any changes.

Tables from older issues

If you create your issues by copying directly from an old issue, you may find that your list of existing tables is empty on first run. This is because the file format changed from .xml to a custom file type - .table - and the list is populated by .table files.

To migrate your old-style tables to the new format, simply open the table editor for each and click Save. You will then see them in the list.

Note that this migration to the new format is not compulsory and your old .xml files will still be displayed correctly.

If you choose to delete a table layer, Creator will ask you if you wish to delete the table's associated file. If you wish to use the table again later in the issue, choose "No". Otherwise, click "Yes" and the file will be deleted from your issue.

Note that table data files which you choose not to delete (and which are therefore no longer associated with a table layer), although remaining in your issue directory, will not be copied into your final built issue. This means that there are no negative side effects to not deleting them, other than cluttering your editing issue directory!

The data file associated with your table layer is shown across the top of the table editor window. You can change the data file which your table layer is associated with by clicking the File button. You will be presented with a list of the table files in that issue - select the file you want your table layer to use. If you select a different file to the one your table layer is currently using, Creator will ask you if you want to delete the file which the table used to reference. As before, select "No" if you want to use it again, and "Yes" if it is no longer needed.

Shared table files

Be careful when changing your table layer's associated data file. If you select a data file which is also referenced by another table layer in your issue, both table layers will then be referencing the same data file. This means that any changes you make to the table data will affect both table layers. It also means that if you choose to delete the table data file if and when one layer no longer needs it, the other layer which references that data file will be left without data and therefore be blank.

Sections, Columns and Rows

Tables are made up of sections, columns and rows. Each section has a section header which floats above the rows of that section as the user is scrolling through the table. Once the user scrolls into a new section, the old section's header is bumped offscreen by the new section's header.

Note that section headers will be hidden if no text has been entered into them.

User-scroll from first to second section

In the table editor, click on Add Section to add a new section to your table, and Add Column to add a column. Rows are added by typing data into the blank row at the bottom of the appropriate section.

Column widths are set as a percentage of the table's width. The first column added is always set to be the full width of the table (100%). As you add more columns in, you will need to adjust your columns' widths so that the total width adds up to 100%. To aid you in this, the table editor shows you each column's width in the section header for that column, as well as the total column width for each section. It will also display an error message if you click on Save Changes when the total width is not 100% in any section. Note that column widths are set per-section to allow for sections with differently-sized columns.

To change a column width, tap on the section header for that column to bring up the column settings menu ( "Column, row and section header settings menus"), and enter the required width in the Width % control.

To delete a section, click on the red Delete button next to the section's header. If the section contains rows, and there are more sections in the table, you will be given the option of deleting the section's rows or simply moving them to an adjacent section.

To delete a row, click on the red Delete button next to the row.

To delete a column, click on the column in any section's header to bring up the column settings menu ( "Column, row and section header settings menus"), and click the Delete button. The column will be deleted from each section.

Rows can be rearranged by clicking and holding the drag handle icon at the end of the row and dragging it to the desired position.

Data Entry

The most common form of data you will be entering into your table is plain text. To enter text, simply click in the relevant cell and type.

To set text in the section header for a column, tap on the header for the appropriate column to bring up the column settings menu ( "Column, row and section header settings menus"), and enter the text in the Title field.

You may want your table cells to display images rather than text. To achieve this, place your images into your issue folder and type the image filename into the appropriate cell, surrounded by square brackets, e.g. [myImage.png]. The image will be aligned to the left of the cell horizontally, and centre-aligned vertically. Any overspill will be clipped to the cell size. Note that images are not supported in section headers - only table cells.

When a table is initially displayed, the data is arranged in the same order in which it was entered by you. When the user taps on the section header for a column, the data in that section and column will be sorted alphabetically. Tapping again will reverse the sort.

Cells which contain image data will be included in the sort operation, using their filenames (including the square brackets) as the data by which to sort. With careful file naming, you can therefore use this feature to have a column containing e.g. star rating images which then sort correctly.

The last type of data which you can enter into cells is sortable dates. If you enter a date value as plain text, it will not necessarily sort in logical date order - alphabetical sorting will be applied to it. To achieve correct date sorting, a special formatting has been introduced: <yyyy.mm.dd.hh.mm.ss>. This formatting (including the angle brackets) will be detected and resolved into a date by which to sort. It will then be displayed in a user-friendly manner (see "Date-formatted entries' display and sort").

The accepted forms of date entry are:

  • <yyyy.mm.dd.hh.mm.ss> - note that you only have to fill this out up to the desired point, e.g. <2012.02> is fine.
  • <Out now>
  • <TBC>
  • <N/A>
  • <Q1>, <Q2> etc - Q represents "quarter".
  • <yyyy.Q1>, <yyyy.Q2> etc.
  • <Spring>, <Summer> etc - both "Autumn" and "Fall" are supported.
  • <yyyy.Spring>, <yyyy.Summer> etc.

The entries that involve words are not case sensitive, so <OUT NOW> is valid, as is <out now> and so on. When it comes to display the value, your casing will be respected.

When the sort operation is performed, the <Out now>, <TBC> and <N/A> entries will be interpreted as a date in the distant past, so that they appear logically in the sequence, e.g. Out now -> in 1 month -> in 2 months etc.

For the sort operation, the season and quarter entries are interpreted as the last day of that season or quarter - which evaluates to the following dates:

  • Q1 - March 31st.
  • Q2 - June 30th.
  • Q3 - September 30th.
  • Q4 - December 31st.
  • Spring - June 20th.
  • Summer - Sept 21st.
  • Autumn / Fall - December 20th.
  • Winter - March 20th the following year (i.e. <2012.Winter> -> March 20th 2013).

Where no years are supplied, the current year at the time the user is viewing it is assumed.

When it comes to displaying your date-formatted entries in the table, the angle brackets will be omitted; your casing will be used, and the following formatting applied:

  • <2012.05.29.10.01.30> - 10:01:30 29 May 2012.
  • <2012.05.29> -> 29 May 2012
  • <Out Now> -> Out Now
  • <TBC> -> TBC
  • <N/A> -> N/A
  • <Q1> -> Q1
  • <2023.Q2> -> Q2 2023
  • <Winter> -> Winter
  • <2023.Winter> -> Winter 2023

Routes

Add a route

The Creator map layer supports route overlays. A route a track imported from a GPS device, or software that supports the standard GPX format. Routes can be used to describe for example how to navigate from one position to another, or the description of a race or other event. GPX is a widely supported XML standard to describe coordinate data. Creator supports routes represented using the track segment of a GPX file only. To import a route from a GPX file, click on the Route text field to select a GPX file to import ( "Add a route"). Note that you may need to scroll and zoom the map to see the route, if the route's extent is far away from the current map centre.

The image used for the pins can be overridden if required. To do so, simply create an image named folioIconPin.png and another named folioIconPin@2x.png and place them inside your issue directory. These files are built into your issue automatically.

If you choose to override the pin image you must provide both the standard- and retina-sized versions. Providing just the standard image would mean that the default image is displayed on retina devices.

For reference, the default pin image is 10 pixels by 10 pixels. You can make your custom image any size and it will be displayed at your size.

Zoom Options

With an info box in the expanded (selected) state, the zoom controls become enabled for that box.

The Zoom slider is used to specify how much the image should be zoomed by when the user taps on the info box. Different info boxes can use different zoom values, as well as zooms of less than 100%, giving a zoom-out effect, if required.

The final control is the Zoom to slider. By default, the zoomed image will attempt to centre itself halfway between the pin and the info box. By manipulating this slider, you can move the zoom point along the line between the pin and the centre of the box. Note that if the pin / box is close to the edge of the layer, it may not be able to be displayed at the centre of the image - it will be displayed as close possible to the centre, while still respecting the layer edges.

While in the expanded mode for a given info box, you can move the box and pin to a more precise location if required, as well as editing the width. You can also click and drag within the annotation layer to see more of the image - just as a user can when viewing your annotation in the expanded mode.

Behaviours

Behaviours are interactivity actions that let the reader control the page. For example, tapping on an image might show some captions, jump to a page, or launch a URL.

A behaviour can be attached to renditions, images, table rows and map pins. In the case of renditions and images, you have more advanced control over your behaviours.

Basic behaviours

The following behaviours are the most commonly used.

Zoom Image

When attached to an image, this allows the reader to tap the image and see it full screen. The full-screen image will be placed onto a black background to dim the page behind, and, if it's a slideshow, animation or 360, will retain its interactivity.

All zoomed images can be dismissed by the reader by tapping the picture.

Parameter: the colour to show behind the zoomed image, defaulting to black. This should be specified as a HTML colour code in the format RGBA (Red, Green, Blue, and Alpha). Examples: 100% black is #000000ff, 100% white is #ffffffff, 50% black is #00000080, a mostly opaque yellow is #ffe700bb.

When activated, this will show or hide a named layer. If the layer is hidden, it will be shown; if it is showing, it will be hidden.

This behaviour allows you to show multiple layers at a time simply by toggling them on with multiple behaviours.

Parameter: the name of the layer to toggle. If multiple layers have the same name, they will all be toggled.

An image with this behaviour attached will be hidden when the user taps on it. A hidden layer will no longer respond to user taps, so this is an effective way to hide a slideshow to reveal something behind it.

Parameter: Not used.

When a user taps an image that has this behaviour attached, the images parent layer will be hidden. This is always used in conjunction with boxes, and allows you to show a box and hide it with very simple interactivity.

A common use for this behaviour is to have a box with multiple layers inside, such as a slideshow, captions for the pictures, and a close button. Rather than have the close button hide just itself (which is what Hide This Layer would do), using Hide Parent Layer would cause the whole box to be hidden, hiding the slideshow, captions and close button all at once.

Parameter: Not used.

Flip animations allow you to hide one view while showing another, which gives the impression of a coin flip on the page. This is commonly used to show that two layers are attached somehow, such as a question and answer.

The basic way to use a flip is to specify the name of the layer to flip to as the behaviour's parameter. For example, if you wanted Image A to flip to Image B, you would attach the behaviour "Transition: Flip Horizontal" to Image A then give it the parameter Image B.

An alternative way to use transitions is to specify to layer names as the parameter, separated by commas. This will flip from the first layer to the second, without necessarily affecting the layer that has the behaviour attached.

Parameter: Either the name of the layer to flip to (in which case, it's assumed the current image is the one to flip from), or the names of the layer to flip from and flip to, separated by commas. If multiple layers have the same name, they will all be flipped.

Show Single Layer

Although this is the most commonly used behaviour in FutureFolio, its use can be confusing so it has a dedicated discussion here.

Show Single Layer allows you to reveal exactly one hidden layer at a time. All layers that started the page hidden will be made hidden, except for the layers you specify as the parameter to this behaviour.

Set a Variable

This lets you set a named variable, either to a specific value (eg 5000 or "Hello"), to a relative value (eg +5000 or -1), or to a list of values (eg "Gears of War", "Halo" and "Mass Effect").

For example: score=5000 will set a variable called "score" to be 5000. If you then say score+5000, it will set score to be 10000 - FutureFolio will take its current value of 5000 and add another 5000 to it.

You can also specify negative values, eg score-5000, which will subtract 5000 from score.

If you wish to make lists of items, such as a wish list or shopping basket, then you can use + and - with text surrounded by quotes. For example, wishlist+"Gears of War" and wishlist+"Halo" will result in both those items being in the wishlist variable. If you use - followed by some text, eg shoppinglist="carrots", all instances of that text will be removed from the list.

When a variable is changed, any Story layers that use variables will be reloaded.

You can assign text to variables simply by typing it in. For example, if you ask the user to choose which games console they want to read about, you could store console=PS3 and check that value later.

If you intend to use your variables to print lists of items, you should know that your variable names can and indeed should be named in a way that means something to the user. That is, "Shopping List" is preferable as a variable name to "shoppinglist".

Parameter: the name of the variable to set, an equals sign, followed by either an absolute value (name=hello); alternatively, the name of the variable to set followed by a relative value (score+5000; score-5000).

Conditions

All image and rendition behaviours can have conditions attached to them that restrict when they can be run. By default, no conditions are applied, which means that your behaviour will always be run. To attach conditions to a behaviour, tap the settings icon next to the behaviour.

If you have attached one or more conditions to a behaviour, all of them must be true in order for the behaviour to run.

To get started, click "Add…". Each condition is made up of three parts: a variable name, a check type, and a value.

  • equals - This is true if the variable is exactly equal to the value. This can be used for numbers (eg score = 4000) or text (eg console = ps3).
  • does not equal - This is true if the variable is not equal to the value. This can be used for numbers or text.
  • is greater than - This is true if the variable is greater than (but not equal to) the value. This can be used for numbers only.
  • is greater than or equal to - This is true if the variable is greater than or equal to the value. This can be used for numbers only.
  • is less than - This is true if the variable is less than (but not equal to) the value. This can be used for numbers only.
  • is less than or equal to - This is true if the variable is less than or equal to the value. This can be used for numbers only.
  • overlaps layer - This is true if the layer name specified under "variable" overlaps the layer name specified under "value" on your page. This can be used with layer names only, and only affects visible layers.

Advanced layer overlaps

When working with the "overlaps layer" condition, you can specify both the variable and value as comma-separated lists of layer names, and if any of those layers overlap then the condition is true. For example, specifying "alien1, alien2, alien3" as the variable and "human1, human2, human3" as the value will evaluate to be true if any alien overlaps any human.

Additionally, if multiple layers have the same name, all of them will be checked for collision.

Timing

When working with behaviours attached to renditions and images, a number of extra features become available to you:

  • Multiple behaviours - You can attach several behaviours at once, and they will be executed in order.
  • "After" - Your behaviour will be executed only after a number of seconds have passed. This defaults to 0
  • "Plays" - The number of times your behaviour will be executed. This defaults to 1.
  • "Delay" - This controls the number of seconds to wait between each repeated play.

So, to make a behaviour that waits a second, then plays five times with a two-second delay between each run, you would set After to be 1, Plays to be 5 and Delay to be 2.

Normally, full-screen slideshows will allow the reader to swipe to the next page when they reach the end of the slideshow, but this functionality becomes disabled when you are using long pages. This also applies to any kind of horizontal scrolling within a page, such as wide pages.

Partial builds

By default, an issue build performs the following actions:

  1. Clears the results of any previous build.
  2. Resize and compress all the image assets you have used.
  3. Copy all assets (images, tables, animations, particle effects, etc) to your build directory.
  4. Copy in your page data (layout, stories and behaviours).
  5. Compress the entire issue and produce a single zip file.

When Partial Build is enabled, steps 1-3 are skipped, which will result in your issue build taking substantially less time than a full build. However, there are a number of provisos:

  1. If you have changed an asset's data (eg changing the contents of a movie, image or animation), it will be ignored.
  2. If you have changed an image's size, it will be ignored.
  3. If you have changed your JPEG quality, it will be ignored.
  4. You must have performed at least one full build before you can perform partial builds.
  5. Your build report will not report any image problems.

Final builds

Never use a partial build for your final issue build, because your zip may include stale content and you may miss important build report warnings.

Creating a chrome

Chromes are essential to all publications, so making it as functional and aesthetic as possible is a must. In this section you'll learn the basics and beyond for making a chrome that compliments your brand.

What is a chrome?

A chrome is an overlaid toolbar that enables the user to navigate your issue easily, access extra features and close that particular issue.

The key to a good chrome is to keep it clean and simple to use and be sure to implement both a navigation method and a Close Issue button, the rest is down to the publication.

Chromes are generated by creating and editing the chrome.xml file. Your chrome can load images from your issue directory and you can have multiple instances for different renditions and devices.

Creating your chrome

Upon creating a blank issue, FutureFolio will automatically generate a chrome.xml file. By default this chrome will load the standard built in chrome that presents a simple constant top bar with a Close button.

To customise your chrome you will need to open the chrome.xml file in your preferred text editor and change the code.

All chrome files star and end with chrome tags, like so:

<chrome>	
	// Your chrome tags here
</chrome>

Inside the chrome tag there are two things you can set:

  1. default - if set to true, ie default="true", and you have created no chrome bars, this will place a dummy chrome on your page to help you test your magazine without first having to design a chrome.
  2. dimAmount - set this to a value between 0 and 1 to control the amount the page should be dimmed when the chrome is opened, where 0 is no dimming at all (the page remains active) and 1 will black out your page entirely.

Inside your chrome tags you need to create bar tags, these will equate to a physical bar that will display in your document.

bar tags have several attributes, some of these are required so that your issue knows where to display your bar and how. Here's a full list:

  • name (required) - This is the unique name of your bar, you can use spaces, but no special characters.
  • xoff (required) - This is the default x (horizontal) position of your bar when it is off (default, unless initialState is set).
  • yoff (required) - Like xoff, this is the initial y (vertical) position of your bar when the bar is off.
  • xon (required) - When your bar is on, this is the x position where your bar will animate to, if you have initialState set to on, then the bar will automatically be set to this.
  • yon (required) - Just like xon, this represents the y position that the chrome needs to animate to when it is activated.
  • width (required) - You need to set the size of your bar, if it's full screen you will need to set this value to the full width of the device. Some titles will use multiple bars that slide in, this is why you need to set the size of each bar.
  • height (required) - Same with the width attribute, this either needs to be the exact height you want the bar to be or the full device size.
  • swipeEnabled - This feature adds up and down swiping to toggle the bar. The user has to be swiping within the on or off area of your bar for the effect to occur, if the entire bar is off the screen this will not work. To enable this, set it to y. To disable this, just do not add the attribute (if this item is set, the app will class it as a y).
  • background (required) - Each bar needs a background image, if you want to handle the imagery with your internal buttons and images, then you may wish to set this as a transparent png file.
  • alphaOff - For a fading bar, you will need to set this value, it's the off state visibility. This requires a number between zero and one, you can use decimal places for mid points, zero being invisible and one being fully visible (so 0.5 would be 50% visible). ideally you'll want to set this as 0 (invisible).
  • alphaOn - If you've set alphaOff, you'll need an on state, this is what the bar will animate to when the bar is toggled on. It uses the same values as alphaOff
  • shouldScroll - If you want your bar to be scrollable, in other words you want to have items in your bar that go further down than the height of your bar, then set this option to y. To disable this, just do not add the attribute (if this item is set, the app will class it as a y).
  • scrollColour - You can change the scroll indicator colour if you want it to be a different colour than the default black one. This ideal for dark coloured chromes. The options are: white or none (For a black indicator, just omit this attribute).
  • initialState - If you want your bar item on by default, you need to set this attribute to on. You can either omit this or set it to off if you do not want to have the bar on by default.
  • appearSound - When activated the bar will play the sound provided. Please note that you can only play audio files in the wav, caf, mp3, aif and aiff file format.
  • disappearSound - If the user closes the bar, this sound will play.

Remember to include your image files in your issue's directory

All images referenced in your chrome need to exist in your issue directory, just like any other image you may use in your issue.

Here's an example of a bar in action.

<chrome>	
	<bar name="top chrome bar" xoff="0" yoff="-150" xon="0" yon="0" width="768" height="200" swipeEnabled="y" background="top_chrome_bar.png">
		// items in your bar here
	</bar>
</chrome>

As you can see, this is a top bar that start 150 points above the top of the screen, showing the bottom 50 points on the screen (here we could display a toggle button). When the bar is on, it moves to the zero y position, showing the full bar.

Inside a bar you have three item types you can add, they are:

  • togglebutton - These are special buttons that toggle the entire chrome. This is essential if you haven't set the initialState attribute of your bar to on.
  • button - The bread and butter of every chrome are the buttons, they activate the various functions in your chrome, from jumping to pages, sections or even sharing text on social networking services.
  • scrubber - A scrubber is a visual navigation device, it's a scrollable area that contains thumbnails of each page. Tapping on a page thumbnail jumps to that page.

All these bar items share some common attributes, they are: x, y, width and height.

The Scrubber has two unique attributes:

  • padding - this sets the number of points of padding each page thumbnail image has on each side of it.
  • centrePages - if this attribute is set or set to "y" it will place the current page in the middle of the scrubber bar, not at the far left (default).

Here's an example of a scrubber bar item in action.

<chrome>	
	<bar name="top chrome bar" xoff="0" yoff="-150" xon="0" yon="0" width="768" height="200" swipeEnabled="y" background="top_chrome_bar.png">
		<scrubber x="20" y="40" width="728" height="80" padding="4" centrePages="y" />
			
		// other items in your bar here
	</bar>
</chrome>

The above creates a centralised scrubber bar that is 40 points down from the top of the bar and stops 20 points from the left and right.

Togglebutton and button items share two attributes that other items do not have:

  • x - this is the horizontal position relative to the top left corner of the bar the item is nested in. So to place an item 80 points in on a screen while in a bar that's 40 points in, you only need to set it's x value to 40 and the bar is already 40 points in from the left.
  • y - again like x, only this is the vertical position on the item relative to the top of the bar it is nested in.
  • up - both button and toggleButton items act like generic interface buttons. The "up" state is the buttons initial state or unselected state. Enter the name of the image file for your button as this attribute.
  • down - when selected or tapped, this is the image state that the button will change to. This needs to be a separate button image if you want it to change on selection. This attribute is optional, you do not require an image for the down state.

With togglebuttons, the up and down attributes can have multiple image filenames separated by a colon (,) character. The image will change based on the section number that you are in.

You can provide a fully transparent PNG image file if you want a tappable region without an image. This can be useful if you want to hide your chrome completely when it is turned off but still enable the user to activate the chrome.

Let's see togglebutton items in action.

<chrome>	
	<bar name="top chrome bar" xoff="0" yoff="-150" xon="0" yon="0" width="768" height="200" swipeEnabled="y" background="top_chrome_bar.png">
		<togglebutton x="0" y="150" width="768" height="50" up="open_arrow.png" down="close_arrow.png" />
		<scrubber x="20" y="40" width="728" height="80" padding="4" centrePages="y" />
			
		// other items in your bar here
	</bar>
</chrome>

In the example, the togglebutton item is always tappable, it's in the bottom 50 points of the top bar.

Regular buttons have further attributes that can be utilised in your chrome. They are the most versatile items in a chrome and can perform many tasks from jumping to a page to closing the entire issue.

Standard button items can have the following attributes:

  • pageSection - this is similar to how the togglebutton item handles section image changes. You can set this attribute to the name of a section, when the user is in this section the button will switch to it's (selected) down status.
  • tapEffectType - You can set your button to use a tap effect, a list of these effects are below.
  • tapEffectData - In most cases this is optional, it is only required if your tap effect needs a value, some data or a command.
  • sound - When tapped the button will play the sound provided. Please note that you can only play audio files in the wav, caf, mp3, aif and aiff file format.

Here is a list of available tap effects and the data required (if applicable) for each one:

  • TAP_JUMPTOSECTION - This jumps the user to the section entered in your tapEffectData attribute.
  • TAP_JUMPTOPAGE - Like jump to section, this jumps the user to a specified page name in your tapEffectData attribute.
  • TAP_PREVIOUSPAGE - Just like an internet browser's Back button, this sends the user to the last page they were on.
  • TAP_NEXTPAGE - Just like an internet browser's Forward button, this sends the user to the last page they jumped backwards from, almost like an undo for the previous page feature above.
  • TAP_TOC - Opens a searchable table of contents from this button. The user can filter down the pages or scroll through the whole issue.
  • TAP_CLOSEISSUE - Closes the issue and returns to the main container app. In FutureFolio Creator it will close the test.
  • TAP_INCREASEFONT - Any live text (not images) will increase in font size. This is global for the entire container app. Please bear this in mind.
  • TAP_DECREASEFONT - You will need this if you wish to enable increase font size, it enables the use to shrink the live text inside your issue.
  • TAP_RESETFONT - Sets the font size back to it's default.
  • TAP_SHAREOPTIONS - Opens a pop over containing any of the three sharing options available in app. You need to set the tapEffectData to any of the following, comma separated for multiple selections, you can have; facebook, twitter or email.
  • TAP_EXTERNALURL - Will open safari with the url provided in the tapEffectData attribute

Animation

Animation scripts in FutureFolio allow you to make layers move around the page, zoom in or out, fade in or out, or spin – or all those things at once. You can set a delay and duration for each animation, animate multiple layers simultaneously, and even create animation chains that allow animations to be executed one after another.

Animation scripts must be created by hand in a text editor, then linked into behaviours using their filename.

Animation Scripts

Animation scripts have a precise, XML-based file format. There is no tool for editing them at this time, so you'll need to edit the XML by hand in your preferred text editor. While this isn't much fun, the results that can be obtained make it worth while!

Before we start, there are some key terms you need to know:

  • Alpha - the opacity of a layere, where 1 is 100% opaque and 0 is 0% opaque. Can be partial values, such as 0.5
  • Angle - the rotation of a layer on the page, expressed in degrees, where 0 its its normal rotation and 90 is rotated 90 degrees clockwise.
  • Animation - one or more timed movements.
  • Curve - how movement accelerates and decelerates. Can be one of "start", "end", "both" and "linear".
  • Delay - the number of seconds an animation will pause before playing.
  • Duration - the number of seconds an animation takes to happen. Can be partial values.
  • Layer - a single movement inside an animation.
  • Scale - the size of a layer on the page, where 1 is 1x its normal size (100%) and 5 is 5x its normal size (500%). Can be partial values.
  • Sequence - chained animations that run in a particular order.
  • X/Y - the position of a layer on the page.

Each animation can contain multiple layer movements, all of which will execute with the same duration and delay.

Each layer movement can affect multiple layers on the page, either because you specified multiple layer names or because several layers have the same name.

All transformations are drawn on the device's graphics card, which is incredibly fast - this allows you to do some fairly advanced effects without worrying about performance.

All values specify where you want a layer to end up, rather than where it's coming from. So, if a layer has scale 2 and you set it to have scale 1, it will scale down; if it has scale 0 then it will scale up. Don't worry about where it is, just specify where you want it to be.

The secret to creating animations is that you only need specify the values you want to change. For example, if you want to make a layer fade out, set its alpha to be 0 - you don't need to set its X, Y, angle or scale values, because they aren't changing.

Working with X and Y

The X and Y co-ordinates of an object combine to form its position, and respond poorly to being manipulated independently. For example, setting Y to 100 over 5 seconds and setting X to 100 over 1 second will not work as you intend: the two positions will clash.

If you simply must animate X and Y independently, the best thing you can do is animate the X value on one layer, place it inside a box, then animate the Y value on the box.

X and Y movements can be specific either as an absolute value, eg x="25", or as a relative value, eg x="x+25" or y="y-100". The former will make the object move to X co-ordinate 25, whereas the latter will add 25 pixels to wherever the layer is right now.

Working with Angles

Any change of angle will be animated using the shortest possible change. For example, if you specify an angle of 270 and the layer currently has an angle of 0, it will rotate 90 degrees anti-clockwise rather than 270 degrees clockwise

By default, rotation of an object occurs around its centre point; see "Anchor Points" for how to modify the point of rotation.

Relative values

Animations values can be manipulated relatively, the following attributes can be altered based on their current value on screen:

  • Alpha
  • Angle
  • Scale
  • X/Y

To use a relative value you need to reference the existing value, this is a matter of entering the name of the attribute inside the new value and one of the following operand:

  • + - This adds to the value before it. so x+10 would place the layer at it's current x position only 10 pixels to the right.
  • - - The opposite of +, so x-10 would move the layer left 10 pixels, whileangle-10 would rotate the layer 10 degrees counter-clockwise.
  • * - Believe it or not, this is the times symbol, so x*1.5 would increase the x value of the layer by 50% of it's x value.
  • / - Finally you can divide the value, so y/1.2 would reduce it's y position by about 83%.

Curves

Your animation curve dictates how your movements happen on the page. There are four options, and the default is "both":

  • linear - your animation will happen at a constant speed.
  • start - your animation will start slowly, then pick up speed.
  • end - your animation will start at full speed, then slow down.
  • both - your animation will start at slowly, pick up speed, then slow down.

The "both" curve gives an S-curve of acceleration and deceleration and is the most visually pleasing.

Examples

A basic animation, to make a layer called Picture1 fade out over three seconds, looks like this:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="0" duration="3">
		<layer name="Picture1" alpha="0" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

You could make it scale up as it fades out by adding a scale change, like this:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="0" duration="3">
		<layer name="Picture1" alpha="0" scale="3" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

Movement can be specified either as an absolute value, like this:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="1" duration="1">
		<layer name="Picture1" x="250" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

Or as a relative value, like this:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="1" duration="1">
		<layer name="Picture1" x="-75" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

You can add multiple layer movements inside the same animation block, such as making one layer fade out and scale up while another fades in and scales down, like this:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="0" duration="3">
		<layer name="Picture1" alpha="0" scale="3" />
		<layer name="Picture2" alpha="1" scale="1" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

Alternatively, if you want to affect multiple layers in the same way, just separate their names with commas, like this:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="0" duration="3">
		<layer name="Picture1, Picture2" alpha="0" scale="3" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

If animations should be executed together, include them all in the same animation block. This animation will fade out and scale up Picture2 over three seconds, while also waiting two seconds and making Picture2 spin 180 degrees:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="0" duration="3">
		<layer name="Picture2" alpha="0" scale="3" />
	</animation>
	
	<animation delay="2" duration="3">
		<layer name="Picture2" angle="180" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

It is important that you understand that these two animations will execute in parallel: the second one will begin while the first is still happening. The delay on the second animation is from the beginning of the animation script, rather than from the end of the first animation. If you want to have one animation wait for another to complete, see "Sequences".

Pre-runs

Pre-run animations allow you to create an animation that runs on a page before the user has seen it, for example if you want to move things off screen or fade them out.

To create a pre-run animation, go to the Rendition Settings window and add a Run Animation Script behaviour that runs your animation with delay 0. In your animation script, you must ensure that your animation has a delay 0 and duration 0.

Any animation that matches these conditions will be executed as soon as the page is created, before it is shown to the user. You can perform any animations you like, and it will all be done instantly.

In addition to the usual collection of animation movements you can choose from, animation pre-runs also allow you to configure the point of rotation for a layer. This defaults to the centre of the layer, meaning that it will rotate as if someone had pushed a pin through its centre. See "Anchor Points" for more information.

Anchor Points

By default, animations are anchored to their centre, so rotation (for example) will spin the object around its centre. This is fine for most purposes, but for advanced animations you might want to have a layer animate around a different point.

If you want to modify an animation's anchor point, please read the following:

  • The anchor point of a layer can only be changed inside an animation pre-run, and only once.
  • This means you should set it to the value you want using a rendition behaviour, then not touch it again.
  • Anchor points are specified as a value relative to the size of the layer: an X value of 0 means the left edge of the layer, an X value of 1 means the right edge of the layer, a Y value of 0 means the top of the layer, and a Y value of 1 means the bottom of the layer.
  • These relative points are used so that you don't have to worry about any scaling that has been applied to the layer: 0 and 1 refer to the edges of the layer regardless of whether it has been scaled.
  • You can use values beyond 0 and 1, and these allow you to rotate your layer from a more distant point. For example, rotating a layer that had an X anchor point of -10 would look like the layer was attached to a large wheel.

In the following example, the anchor point of the Picture1 layer is set to its top-left corner, then the layer is scaled up to 3x its normal size:

		
<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="0" duration="0">
		<layer name="Picture1" anchorX="0" anchorY="0" />	
	</animation>
	
	<animation delay="1" duration="3">
		<layer name="Picture1" scale="3" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

Sequences

Animation sequences allow you to create animation chains that execute one after another, and are the best way to create complex animations where one movement should execute only if another has finished.

Animation sequences are created in two parts: naming animations and using the onFinish attribute for animations.

A named animation looks like this:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="2" duration="3" sequenceName="myanim">
		<layer name="Picture1" alpha="0" />
	</animation>	
	
	â¦more animationsâ¦
</animationBlock>

That animation will not be executed until something else in the same animation script tells it to run.

Sequenced animations won't auto-execute

Do not give your animations a sequence name if you want them to run automatically. Any animation with a sequence name will not be executed until it is forced to execute by another animation in the same script.

Execute a named animation is done by adding an onFinish attribute to an animation, like this:

<animationBlock>
	<animation delay="1" duration="1" onFinish="myanim">
		<layer name="Picture1" scale="2" />
	</animation>		
	
	<animation delay="2" duration="3" sequenceName="myanim">
		<layer name="Picture1" alpha="0" />
	</animation>	
</animationBlock>

You can trigger multiple animations using onFinish, either by specifying multiple sequence names separated by commas (eg, onFinish="a, b, c") or by giving more than one animation the same sequence name. For example, if you use onFinish="myanim" and there are two animations named "myanim", both will be executed.

Delays in animation sequences are based on the time they were started, rather than the time the whole animation script started. As a result, the above animation script will execute like this:

  1. After one second, make Picture1 scale up to 2x its size over a period of one second.
  2. When that has finished, wait a further two seconds, make Picture1 fade out over a period of three seconds.

Although you could do that without sequences, using them guarantees you precise timing.

Optimising your Issue

Keeping your issue size small is critical to ensuring reader satisfaction, which means you absolutely must think about it.

To be clear: readers have a very strong dislike of large magazines, because they take a long time to download - sometimes hours - and take up a lot of space on their device. The only way to get issue size down is to be smart from the beginning, so please read this section carefully.

If your final issue size is over 400MB, expect some readers to complain. If your final issue size is over 600MB, expect many readers to complain.

JPEG vs PNG

The most effective way to lower your issue size is to create your images in the correct format. Nearly all images in FutureFolio Creator should be in one of two image formats:

  • JPEG: best for photos; must have no transparency.
  • PNG: best for highly important pictures, pictures of text, or any image requiring transparency.

When you run a build, Creator allows you to adjust the compression of JPEGs, but it will leave PNG images untouched.

JPEG images will, in nearly all circumstances, be smaller files than PNG images, and at higher compression levels the difference could be 5x or more. As a result, we strongly recommend that you use JPEG for all your photographic images.

JPEG compression can create some artefacting that may be unpleasant in certain kinds of images, particularly if the picture contains text. If you run a build and find the JPEG compression is unpleasant on some pictures, you should consider changing them to PNG on a case-by-case basis.

Similarly, you may wish to use PNG for some images if they are standing graphics used several times in your issue and you want to ensure the image remains punchy.

JPEG quality

When laying out your pages, you should ensure your JPEGs have as little compression applied as possible. This is because Creator will recompress all your JPEGs when you do a build, which means all your assets remain at maximum quality until the last moment.

When you do a build, you will be prompted to select a JPEG quality setting. Although it is tempting to choose 100% under the rationale "we want our images to look good, right?", the reality is that such a setting is counterproductive.

Because of the way JPEG works, a setting of 100% will actually create an image that has a larger file size than its original, with no improvement in quality. In fact, any quality over 85% will look almost identical, even though an image at 95% quality will be much larger than an 85% quality image.

We suggest that you start your JPEG quality low – 33% is a fairly safe starting point – then build your issue and see how it looks, only increasing the quality as needed. If you find yourself using a JPEG quality above 75%, you will experience diminishing returns: file sizes will increase dramatically with little noticeable improvement in quality.

Inspecting the Build Directory

When editing your pages, you should be using full-size images with the minimum of compression applied. When you perform a build, FutureFolio Creator will resize your images to the size they are used on your pages, and, if they are JPEGs, compress them to the setting you have chosen. It will also generate retina-quality images for iPad 3 and other retina-compatible devices.

When the build is finished, Creator will produce a zip file of your issue, and you'll be able to see how big your finished issue is. It will also produce a build directory, showing you all the files that went into the zip. If your issue size is unexpectedly large, the best place to start is to examine this build directory: sort all the files by size, and see which ones take up the most space.

If you find you have several files taking up a large part of your issue, you will find it most effective to try to lower the sizes of these files first, before trying to optimise the smaller files.

Keeping your directories separate

Your issue build directory and your issue working directory are kept separate on your file system, and you should never mix the two.

The reason for this is because when you do a build, your images are scaled down and compressed, ready for use on the device. If you then use this built version of your issue for editing, you'll be recompressing compressed pictures, resulting in a drastic reducation of quality.

Always, always ensure that you keep your issue working directory separate from your build directory.

Videos

Videos can take up a disproportionately large amount of space in your issue, and there are two ways to correct this:

  1. Reduce the quality: although the iPad as a result of 1024x768 or 2048x1536 depending on the model, it is not necessary to have your videos at this resolution. Low-resolution pictures look soft in the screen, but movies – by their very nature – move, and so the lower resolution is less obvious to the eye. Our tests have shown that video resolutions as low as 512x384 look perfectly fine even on retina iPads.
  2. Stream your videos: if you have compressed your videos and they still take up too much space, consider streaming your videos from the web. These can be placed and used in Creator the same way as local movies, but they take up no space at the cost of requiring the reader to be connected to the internet.

You can mix and match local and streaming videos. If your video is required for the page, eg a video for your cover or some other important footage, then you can include it in the issue and let others stream.

Advanced Techniques

This part of the guide explains a handful of techniques that rely on putting several simpler things together, or are less commonly used.

Controlling the Page with Slideshows, Animations and Boxes

To avoid overwhelming the user with information on a single page, it's possible to show and hide layers as the user interacts with a slideshow, animation, 360, carousel or scrolling box.

To make this technique work, you need to name your layers in a very specific way. For example, if the slideshow, animation or box is called "mypics", you need to name the layers you want to show and hide "mypics-1", "mypics-2", and so on. You should then hide all the layers that you want start as being invisible, which, in our example, would be everything after "mypics-1".

As the user swipes through slideshow pages (or as the animation advances, etc), the correct layer attached to "mypics" is shown. For example, page three of the slideshow will show "mypics-3". The same is true of animations: when the third frame of the animation is shown, "mypics-3" will be shown. FutureFolio will automatically hide all the other "mypics-" objects so that only one is showing at a time.

This technique is often used to show captions that relate to a specific picture in the slideshow or animation, but you can show or hide any layers this way - boxes full of information, for example. You can also show and hide multiple layers by giving them the same name, eg you can have five layers called "mypics-1".

If you want to use scrolling boxes to control the page, you should layout the box so that its content scroll off the screen horizontally, choose the option "Horizontal" under "Scroll to fit contents" (not "Both"!), then make sure "Scroll in pages" is set to either "On" or "Paging dots".

Controlling the Page with Stories

You can show and hide layers as the user scrolls through a story layer by placing special markers in your text. These markers tell FutureFolio the name of the layer you want to show, and internally it uses a Show Single Layer effect (see "Show Single Layer") so that only one hidden layer is shown at a time.

You can place as many markers in your text as you want; FutureFolio will automatically find the one nearest the top of the page and show it.

Here's an example of marker layers in action:

<marker layer="layer1" />
Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit. Nunc sed lorem magna.
Quisque id lectus et ligula commodo vestibulum. Nulla id eros sed urna ornare lobortis.
Vivamus tellus mauris, mollis in lobortis dignissim, vulputate quis leo. 
<marker layer="layer2" />
Duis pretium dapibus tristique. Etiam in mauris eu erat molestie convallis.
Duis convallis porta nibh, ut iaculis enim venenatis vitae.
Nullam ipsum leo, sodales et ornare id, dictum ac dui.
<marker layer="layer3" />
Vivamus sodales nisi quis odio rutrum id hendrerit metus dignissim.
Cras vitae leo a lacus vestibulum dapibus. Maecenas posuere, est sed molestie
ultricies, purus libero cursus neque, quis euismod orci magna vitae elit.

That example has three markers. As the reader scrolls down the page, first layer1 will be shown, then layer2, then layer3.

This technique is commonly used for pages that have long-form reading – you can show images that illustrate what the user is reading about right now rather than clutter the page up with many images.

It can also be used to detect when the user has started reading a story. You can show any layer you want using this system, so if, for example, your story was a review of a game in the App Store, you could make a button appear saying "Buy this on the App Store!" as soon as the user scrolls the story slightly, on the basis that scrolling the story means they are probably reading it.

Particle Effects

FutureFolio can create special effects commonly seen in video games, such as fire, smoke, fog, steam, rain and snow. These effects are called particle effects, because the special effects system is made up of hundreds (or perhaps even thousands) of tiny particles that move around the page in a way that, combined, looks like the effect you're trying to produce.

Particles are immensely powerful, which means there's a learning curve required to take full advantage of them. You have complete control over how the particles behave, which means it's possible to create your own custom effects very easily.

The best way to create particles in Creator is to use the Particle Effects editor window, which is located under the page editor – tap the Effects button then choose Particle Effects.

Below is a description of what the various controls do. Any value marked "range" allows you to specify a range of values (for example, a Red value of 128 and a Red Variation value of 10 means that any red value between 108 and 138 can be used).

  • X - the horizontal position where the particle system should be created.
  • Y - the vertical position where the particle system should be created.
  • Width - the width of the particle system; this is not used if you choose Line shape.
  • Height - the height of the particle system; this is not used if you choose Line shape.
  • Shape - whether particles should be created from a single point, a rectangle or a line.
  • Mode - "normal" mode will show overlapping particles with no special blending; "additive" will increase the brightness of overlapping particles that have have an alpha value below 1.
  • Birth Rate - how many particles to create every second. Try to keep this as low as possible.
  • Lifetime - how long in seconds the particle system should continue to make particles.
  • Particle Lifetime - how long in seconds each particle should live
  • Particle Lifetime Range - how long in seconds to vary the length of each particle's life
  • Images - a collection of images to use for your particles; one of these will be randomly chosen for each particle.
  • Container Layer - if specified, particles will be created inside this layer on your page; this allows you to control their exact position above or below other layers.
  • Red - the starting red colour of each particle, specified as 0-255 (0 = no red; 255 = max red)
  • Green - the starting green colour of each particle
  • Blue - the starting blue colour of each particle
  • Alpha - the starting alpha value of each particle (0 = transparent; 255 = fully opaque)
  • Red Range - the amount that the red colour should vary from particle to particle
  • Green Range - the amount that the blue colour should vary
  • Blue Range - the amount that the blue colour should vary
  • Alpha Range - the amount that the blue colour should vary (negative alpha values and alpha values over 255.0 are forced into the range 0.0 - 255.0.)
  • Alpha Speed - the amount that the alpha value of each particle should change over its lifetime. This should be specified as a value between -255 and 255. See below for details.
  • Velocity - how fast each particle should move
  • Velocity Range - the amount the speed should vary from particle to particle
  • Horizontal Acceleration - how much the horizontal speed should change over time (see below for details)
  • Vertical Acceleration - how much the vertical speed should change over time (see below for details)
  • Direction - the direction, in degrees, to create particles, where 0 is directly upwards and 90 is 90 degrees to the right.
  • Direction Range - the amount of varation of particle creation direction, in degrees; a value of 90 will create a 90-degree cone around your direction.
  • Spin - the number of rotations each particle should perform over its life time; specify negative values for anti-clockwise rotation.
  • Spin Range - the amount to vary the spin value from particle to particle
  • Scale - how large each particle should be created; the default is 1, which means its normal size.
  • Scale Range - how much to vary the size of each particle.
  • Scale Speed - how much to modify each particle's scale over its lifetime.

When you've created your particle system, enter a filename into the box in the bottom-left corner of the screen, and tap Save. You can also load a pre-saved particle system for editing by tapping the Load a Saved Effect button.

Alpha Speed

This value affects how your particles change their alpha value during their lifetime. If, for example, your particle is created with an alpha of 128 (50% transparent), setting an alpha speed of -128 will make the article fade out to be invisible as it ages.

Before you use your particle system, we recommend you tap Preview to ensure that it looks and works as you intended.

Once your particle system has been created, you can create it using the "Create Particles" behaviour and specifying your particle filename as the behaviour's parameter.

If you wish to have one particle image be used more than the others, simply specify it more times. For example, adding Image1 three times and Image2 once will cause Image1 to be used 75% of the time.

Particle performance

Particle effects are rendered on the device's graphics card, which means they are very fast. However, there are three exceptions:

  1. Having a high birth rate and/or having large particle graphics.
  2. Using multiple particle systems on a page, or on adjacent pages.
  3. Running in the iOS Simulator.

We strongly recommend you avoid using multiple particle systems on adjacent pages - leave at least a one-page gap between particle systems to ensure they all run at maximum speed.

Although the iOS Simulator does a very good job of simulator a real iPad, particle systems run slower in the virtual iPad than they do in a real iPad.

Dynamic movement

There are two effects you can enable that result in your page layout changing in unpredictable ways, and these are enabled on a per-layer basis by setting "Dynamic Movement" to be on for your layer.

Dynamic movement comes in two forms: accelerometer tilting and physics. It is important that you do not mix tilting, physics or animation, as they will fight for control over the position of your layers.

Reading the tilt sensor

You can create some interesting page effects, not least faux depth, by enabling the device's accelerometer. This will read the user's movement of the device as they tilt it left, right, forwards and backwards, and send that movement data to your page so that layers are shifted to give the user a different perspective on the page.

To enable the accelerometer for a page, use the behaviour "Enable accelerometer" either as a rendition behaviour or an image behaviour and give it a number for its parameter. This number affects the speed that should be shifted as the user moves their device, and it defaults to 1. You can set large values (eg 10) to allow the user to reveal things by moving their device.

When the "Enable accelerometer" behaviour has been triggered, all layers on the current page that have Dynamic Movement enabled will respond to the user's tilting actions.

FutureFolio will move each object at varying speeds depending on their position on your page: objects at the back of the page will move slowest and objects at the front will move fastest.

Objects inside boxes will move inside the box, even if the box itself doesn't have dynamic movement enabled. The speed of each object is determined by the position of its parent box, and its position inside its parent box.

Be careful with the accelerometer

Please read the following provisos with tilt behaviours:

  • Once enabled, the accelerometer can't be disabled for that page unless the user scrolls a couple of pages away and returns.
  • The accelerometer will move any dynamic movement layers; if you try to animate or use physics on these layers while they are moving, you will not get the effect you want. Do not try to animate or use physics with tilt-enabled objects once the accelerometer has been enabled for the page.
  • Only one page can have control over the accelerometer at a time. If you intend to have two adjacent pages that use the accelerometer, make sure you place an image on the page that allows the user to re-activate the accelerometer for whichever page they choose.

Working with Physics

One of the most "interesting" special effects in Folio is the creation of a physics engine. This is done by attaching the behaviour "Enable Physics" either to a rendition or to an image behaviour, and will cause all layers that have "Responds to Physics" enabled to respond as if your page had real-life physics.

The physics engine in FutureFolio is fairly simple: layers will fall to the bottom of the page by default, and will bounce off or rest on any other layers that they touch that are also enabled for physics.

If you want to create a physics force when the user taps on something, use the "Create Physics Force" behaviour and specify the strength of the physics force as the behaviour's parameter. This will have the effect of a "bomb" on the page - nearby objects will zoom away as they are pushed by your force.

Be careful with physics

Please read the following provisos with physics:

  • Once enabled, physics can't be disabled for that page unless the user scrolls a couple of pages away and returns.
  • Physics will move any physics-enabled layers based on their gravity and other layers; if you try to animate these layers while they are moving, you will not get the effect you want. Do not try to animate or use tilt effects with physics-enabled objects once physics has been enabled for the page.
  • Two objects that are enabled for physics that start life overlapping on your page will exhibit strange behaviour as they resolve their overlap.
  • All objects use their layer frames (ie, the box you've drawn for them) as their collision shapes.

Draggable objects

Draggable objects in FutureFolio allow your user to interact with the page by dragging objects around the page. You can control which objects are draggable and when, you can also apply conditions to draggable objects to execute effects.

Be careful with animation scripts

You can run an animation script on a draggable object whilst a user is dragging it, however if you translate that object in a different direction to which they move it you may cause some undesired effects.

Respect the layer hierarchy

Draggle objects respect the layer hierarchy of the page, this means the user can drag an object behind another layer and release it. If they do this and you have not enabled pass-through-taps on that layer then they will not be able to interact with the draggable object again until the page reloads.

Draggable object types

All layer types that support dynamic movement are draggable at run-time.

These currently are:

  • Boxes
  • Stories
  • Images

Enabling draggable objects

Any object you wish to be draggable at run-time must have its "Dynamic movement" property set to ON.

To enable all draggable objects on a page add "Enable draggable objects" to the pages renditions setting panel and set the "Plays" parameter to "1".

To enable draggable objects as the action of a tap effect, add "Enable draggable objects" as a tap effect to a layer. The "Value" parameter specifies the layer names that you wish to become draggable (comma separated), and again the "Plays" parameter must be set to "1".

Draggable objects can not be disabled

Once enable draggable objects will remain draggable until the page is unloaded.

Working with conditions

Draggable objects work with the "overlaps layer" condition "Conditions"

Remember the layer hierarchy

Organise your layers to achieve the effect you desire, if layers are not organised correctly then conditions may not be valid.

Hybrid editions

In some instances a publication may only wish to create a fully interactive edition for a specific device but without losing the subscriber base of other devices. Hybrid editions solve this issue by providing a digital replica option within the issue for non-supported devices.

Digital replicas in interactive editions

So you've created an amazingly detailed interactive publication and spent a vast amount of time perfecting it for a specific device, but don't have the time, resource or desire to create a rendition for the opposite device. On the same note you have many subscribers on both devices and don't want to lose users, so what's the solution? A simple digital replica that appears for the other device.

Adding a digital replica to your interactive edition is incredibly simple. Take your digital replica and unzip it. Rename the folder to sd and copy that folder into your built issue. The app will handle loading it automatically if your interactive edition does not support that device.

In-App Purchasing

In-App Purchasing (IAP) is the mechanism by which you monetize your product. Apple takes much of the pain out of purchasing, including the handling of credit card payments, collection of tax, which is part of the service they provide for the 30% revenue cut they take. You decide how much to charge for single issues, and for subscriptions, within a tiered pricing structure that Apple provides. There are no hard and fast rules on how much you should or shouldn't charge for your product. You know your readership and your market and probably have a feel for what will and what won't be acceptable.

Single Issues

Single issues need to follow Apple's pricing tiers, but may vary from issue to issue. Pricing can also be changed such that a new issue is initially priced higher/lower for a period of time for promotional reasons. Several models exist in terms of pricing strategy. Some magazines have been successful in combining a high single issue price with a heavily discounted, short subscription term, say a 30 day term costing significantly less than the cost of a single issue. See "Pricing Tiers" for details on the pricing tiers.

Subscriptions

Subscriptions follow the same pricing tiers as single issue sales (including free). See "Pricing Tiers" for details on the pricing tiers. Subscriptions are by default auto-renewing, regardless of term. If a customer takes up a 30 day subscription, this amounts to a life-time commitment which will only terminate when they explicitly cancel. The number of days in the term is the number of days between renewals, not the length of the subscription.

Apple's subscription terms are all time-based, rather than issue-based. They are either 1-day, 7 days, 30 days, 90 days, 180 days or 365 days. A time-based subscription term can be a challenge to explain to the user, as a fixed time period can give a different number of issues depending on the start date.

Note that App revenue is usually boosted by being featured on one of Apple's App Store lists, such as Top Paid, Top Grossing etc. This is worth bearing in mind when considering pricing structure. A subscription model that renews more frequently will bump up the revenue more frequently, and may sustain an app better by keeping it in the Top Grossing list.

Changing the subscription price

Ensure that you never increase the cost of an existing subscription, as this will switch off the auto-renewals for those on this option.

Asset requirements

Every app on the iTunes App Store has a number of required assets such as an app icon, a launch splash screen, app name, description and other data. Together these requirements make up your app's app store entry and stipulate where it's located, how well it is found and the impact on the end user.

Once your details and images have been created you will need to send them to your account manager for inclusion and usage.

App icons

The app icon is a square iconic representation of your app. There are a number of apple guidelines on what the icon can and can't contain and a few tips on how to note get rejected for your app icon available from the Apple Developers website. The abridged version is that you cannot use copyrighted imagery that is not your own, it cannot contain anything explicit and each icon must be unique. You cannot use the same icon for multiple apps.

Although App Store Newsstand has in effect rendered the icon useless for Newsstand apps, an app icon is still required for submission to Apple. You will also need this for pre iOS 5.0 firmware which at the time of writing is still in use. Another thing to consider is if Apple change how they display Newsstand products in the future, you may well be caught out if you don't take the icon seriously.

To create your app icon, you will need to provide two - four images, depending on what devices you wish to support:

  • iPad icon (file name: Icon-72.png) - Your standard resolution iPad icon, this will display for non-newsstand apps and on devices using a firmware version earlier than iOS 5.0. The dimensions are 72 X 72.
  • iPad icon Retina (file name: Icon-72@2x.png) - This is the retina version of the iPad icon. This should look like the standard one, only it's twice the size at 144 X 144.
  • iPhone icon (file name: Icon.png) - iPhones have smaller screens, so the standard icon is 57 X 57.
  • iPhone icon Retina(file name: Icon@2x.png) - The retina version for iPhone 3Gs' onwards is 114 X 114.

All filenames are case sensitive and must match the above exactly.

iTunes App Store icons are rounded by default, the store does this for you, it is suggested you do not attempt to do this yourself. Transparency is not supported in your icon and you can optionally have the app icon shine added by apple, it's again suggested you don't add your own shine unless you are sure you have disabled the apple built in shine.

The @2x files are not an opportunity to display a different icon, both standard and retina icons should be the same icon completely, only the retina version is a higher resolution.

App splash screen

As your app loads a full screen splash screen is displayed. This image is entirely custom, you can display anything you wish here as long as it doesn't break any of Apple's terms and conditions or guidelines. The retina version needs to match the non-retina image also.

  • iPad splash portrait (file name: Default-Portrait.png/Default-Portrait@2x.png) - Standard: 768x1024 Retina: 1536x2048
  • iPad splash landscape (file name: Default-Landscape.png/Default-Landscape@2x.png) - Standard: 1024x768 Retina: 2048x1536
  • iPhone splash portrait (file name: Default.png/Default@2x.png) - Standard: 320x480 Retina: 640x960

Brand logos

Loitered throughout the app your logo will be visible, it's important that your logo is clear, not cluttered and in keeping with the style o the app.

The top bar where your brand will be located is always dark grey fading to black, so make sure your logo is bright and works with a dark background. A standard theme in FutureFolio based apps is the use of solid white icons and logos on the top bar, it's suggested that you follow this.

As for your artwork files, you will need to create two - four as with the app icon:

  • iPad logo (file name: logo.png) - Your main iPad logo, it needs to be 32 pixels tall, the width is variable, but please refrain from extremely long images as it may interfere with other design elements.
  • iPad logo retina (file name: logo@2x.png) - As with all retina images, this image needs to be identical only twice the size. This image needs to be 64 pixels tall.
  • iPhone logo (file name: logo-iPhone.png) - Like the iPad logo this file needs to be 32 pixels tall. Remember that the iPhone is a lot slimmer than an iPad, you may want to make the image somewhat thinner to fit correctly in the limited space available on the device.
  • iPhone logo retina (file name: logo-iPhone@2x.png) - The same image as the non-retina version, this time it's 64 pixels tall.

App Metadata

The Apple iTunes App Store displays an unique page for each app, this is your window to the world and the page that will make or break your app. The data and images on this page are handled by your provided app metadata and uploads.

In this section we will cover some essential tips, tricks and guides on making the most of your metadata while still following the Apple guidelines and maximising the possibility of your app getting past the Apple review process that each app has to go through.

Naming your app

Currently your app's name is it's number one influence on your search result ranking in the Apple iTunes App Store. Your app's name needs to match the exact word you wish to rank for if you want to maximise the effect, no extra words, just the word you want to be top for. This isn't ideal for most publications as they wish to maintain a brand image. Also it's useless to rank for something if your app would be of no interest to that audience.

Apple has recently taken a strong stance against the opposite spectrum of app name ranking, "keyword stuffing", these are apps that stuff many relevant keywords into their title, they are now being rejected on a regular basis.

Finally, all app names must be unique, if someone else got your app name before you then you will have to settle for an alternative name or appending "magazine" or "mag".

Writing your app description

App descriptions are automatically collapsed roughly after the first paragraph, so ideally in that space you will want to sell your app's functionality to the best of your ability.

Your description is plain text, however the Apple emoji icons still work if you know how to use them and wish to add to your description.

In the app review process the description will be read and you can fail on the data entered here alone. You cannot infringe on copyrights in the space or claim any relation to Apple either directly or indirectly (i.e. through a competition prize).

Mastering keywords

The second biggest factor that affects your search ranking are your app keywords. In 100 characters you can stipulate as many comma separated keywords as you wish, the character count includes all comma characters and spaces, so be selective and pick the most common words that you wish to rank for. Also remember that all keywords have to be checked by Apple.

There's no way to "game" the keywords by duplicating the same word, duplication are merely ignored.

App screenshots

Screenshots are a requirement for any and all apps on the iTunes App Store, they define your apps supported device list, so if you want your app to be universal (iPad and iPhone) you need to provide both iPad and iPhone screenshots. If either set of screenshots are missing then the app upload created by the App Development Team will fail.

Screenshots will only upload if they are the correct size

Your screenshots will not upload if they are not the correct dimensions for the device you are uploading for. Please use actual screenshots to avoid this issue. Here is a breakdown of the device screenshot sizes:

  • iPad - 768 X 1024 (Retina: 1536 X 2048)
  • iPhone - 320 X 480 (Retina: 640 X 960)

Your screenshots are the quickest and easiest way to show off and sell your app to potential customers. It's essential that you pick the most aesthetically pleasing app screens to promote your app. Any key features of your app ideally need to be showcased here.

To take a screenshot it is recommended you run your app on a device and use the Home and Close button combo to take your screenshots. This will ensure the screenshots are the correct size.

Screenshots only not as you know them

You may have seen on the app store that some apps use the screenshot images to promote their app without being pure screenshots, they may contain two or more screenshots within them with some nuggets of information. This can be utilised to express features that cannot be seen in a single screenshot alone. However this is down to Apple's discretion as to if they will let your faux screenshot through.

Video - supported types

iOS devices only support a handful of video formats, they are:

  • mov
  • mp4
  • m4v

Any other format will need to be converted into one of these formats in order to work in app.

Only a select number of video codecs are supported on iOS devices also, they are:

  • H-264
  • MPEG-4

It is recommended that you keep the bitrate to a minimum, this is subjective however as it's down to what quality drop you are willing to accept.

Finally for resolution we recommend 720p, you can create your videos at 1080p, however older devices will struggle with video in that resolution.
